Session 5

Tinkering is a way to generate knowledge. What kind of knowledge results from a tinkering process in your experience?

Tinkering is much more adept at generating an understanding of how something works, or at least how to work with something. It is not very suitable for generating 'factual information' knowledge.

Tinkering with classical materials like clay, paper, colour, textiles is more related to craft. How does tinkering work with digital and electronic media?

If we are to take the defenition of tinkering as provided to us by the course, then tinkering with digital media simply doesn't work as you cannot touch it. With electronic media there is a possibility for more hands on capabilities in the sense of constructing the circuitry. But at a sufficiently high skill level or when you want to branch off from the few things that tinkering kits might allow you to do, a lot of time will be spent coding instead of being hands on with the parts.

Design practice changed during the past years, from long design phases to short, iterative processes, due to the availability of new prototyping technologies. Is this iterative process of prototyping qualitatively different from the cycles we do in tinkering?

Yes. Iterative design is often times focused on an optimalisation problem, to get the best of one specific thing. While Tinkering is much more focused on discovery. Iterative design starts with (defining) a concrete goal, while the goal with tinkering is often a much more general one.

Can a co-design session be described as a tinkering session as we treat it in our course?

That depends a little on how the session is set up. A co-design session can very much be set up as a tinkering session, where the intended outcome are the new ideas that the participants come up with. The focus will also be less on the educating part like it is with most of our designs, but rather on the exploration of the concepts and possible designs.

The choice of materials (seed, tools, scaffolding, facilitation) has an effect on the outcome, the knowledge and products made in a tinkering session. How can we use that in a co-design session? Can we really influence the kind of results here, and do we want that?

The preciousness of the materials will play a big role in what the participants will make. If its less precious participants are more likely to make a lot of quick itterations, possibly only fulfilling one criteria, and then combine them. If the materials are more preciouss, the participants are more likely to try and make one thing that fulfills all criterias from the start. I would generally say that the first is more preferable, as it becomes easier to test the influence of the individual criterias and it also gives a better idea of what criterias the participants consider most important. Once material has been selected, the appropriate tools for it should be provided. Here, a balance must be struck between allowing the participants to do what they want with the material without overwhelming them with the options. The seed will lay in the prompt(s) and the scaffolding and other facilitation will depend on what the design requires.

The ideal infuencing of the results in my opinion would be a comparmentilisation of features, where the participants have created different sub-systems that we can then test seperately. A worst case scenario for co-design would be a "penguin paradox" as Edwin talked about in one of his lectures, where an example is given and everyone simply tries to replicate the example.

In “research through design” can tinkering fill in the design part?

This again depends on what research needs to be completed. If the research aims to find general information about a certain concept, then I can defenetly see tinkering being used to design whatever thing is required for the research. However, if the research is more comparitive, if it tries to determine the effect of a singular independent variable, then thinkering is less usefull then theoretically reasoned options. In part as well because there simply isn't that much space to tinker in a research like this.

Are there design questions where tinkering is not a possible or useful approach?

yes, as explained previously, when the goal of the research is to determine the effect or nature of a single or specific set of variables, then tinkering becomes less usefull as it becomes a lot harder to isolate all the other effects generated from the designs. In general, I would argue that descriptive, explanatory and correlational research [1] are less suitable for tinkering in their approach. The design questions of these categories are all too specific and constrained for tinkering. This would include design questions such as:

  • How do people react to X?
  • Why do people react to X in Y way?
  • How do people react to X, if we change Z?

[1] DiscoverPhDs, Types of research - Explained with examples, in DiscoverPhDs Community blog, 02/10/2020, Available: https://www.discoverphds.com/blog/types-of-research, Last accessed 13/04/2025.

Read Invent to Learn page 41. how to balance real-world (criticality) with the fruitful mindset of tinkering?

This question doesn't make much sense to me as worded, but this is what I think it asks: How do you create a balance between the free mindset of tinerking and the application in real-world scenarios that have a lot of requirements and optimalisation?

For this, thinkering can be a first tool to get general ideas flowing, as well as a tool to learn about possible materials to use in the final designs. If you were to use the creative technology design process [2], tinkering could be used during the ideation phase as the tool to generate concepts. Then, during the specification phase the real world and its requirements shape these ideas that originated from tinkering into something suitable for the design.

[2] A. Mader and W. Eggink. “A Design Process for Creative Technology”. In: Proceedings of the 16th International conference on Engineering and Product Design, EPDE 2014. Ed. by E. Bohemia et al. EPDE. 16th International Conference on Engineering and Product Design, EPDE 2014; Conference date: 04-09-2014 Through 05-09-2014. what is the critical impact your tinkering exercise could have? (on you, your design/engineering practice or problem, both positive and negative)

The main goal of our tinkering workshop will be to make the students following it more aware of the capabilities of paper and origami in robotics. This could lead to quicker tinkering with new designs, lighter designs and most importantly, novel designs that would not work as well with more conventional robotics. The two major downsides to origami as compared to more traditional robotics however are the fact that paper and cardboard are less structurally sound then say plastic or aluminium, and through experimentation we found out that a lot of action origami is based on the spring like effect of paper folds, but this effect weakens over time. Thus, origami robots would most likely not last as long as standard robots.

what is the impact your session have regarding (more) stuff, ecological footprint and impact on our planet (i.e. how can you avoid that STEM workshops with waste material result in more waste material?)

Given the fact that these robots are not likely to last very long, there would be more waste. Furthermore, the development of origami robots generally also requires more itterations as it is somewhat hard to wrap your head around the mechanics of how it all works. However, the large majority of this will be paper waste, which can be recycled. The mechanical parts will most likely wear at the same rate as in normal robotics. But this time, less metals are needed to make the robots work. So as long as not too many extra trees have to be cut down to make all the paper, I think it would have a marginally positive influence on the climate. Paper is also a lot cheaper then more conventional materials, which allows more people to get a start with robotics then before.
