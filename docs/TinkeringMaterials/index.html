Tinkering Materials

Materialease of usepreciousnessmouldabilityinterfacingpreknowledge requirements
LegoLego bricks are very quick and easy to both assemble and disassemble though their snapping mechanism, while at the same time retaining quite a bit of structural integrety.Lego has somewhat created a culture around itself that you do not destroy, glue or otherwise alter the bricks themselves. This is most likely because this reduces reusability and because the brick you wanted probably already existed.Due to the variety of the bricks available lego can be used to make almost anything. The main thing that lego still struggles with is structural support for pulling forces, but this can be overcome with techinic bricks.The only thing that is stopping people from glueing the bricks to other materials is the notion that "you don't do that". Cardboard, paper, wood, etc. can also be attached to the lego technic pieces quite easily though split pins, steel cables or nails.The simple snapping mechanism of both system and technic makes them rather intuative to use.
CardbordCardboard might be one of the easiest tinkering materials to use. Its sturdy enough to make complex 3 dimensional shapes while not being so sturdy that advanced tools are needed to use the material.While it might feel wrong to cut up pristine cardbord boxes, anything that has a tear, cut, print or other deformation on it generally has the feeling that it is trash already so no value would be lost though its destruction.Cardboard can take on a huge veriaty of shapes and forms while keeping structural integrety. The only thing limiting the mouldabilty of the cardboard are the tinkerers skill level and maybe the matterials used to create attachments.Interfacing with cardboard can be done in many ways. And because the cardboard has such a low preciousnes it is fine if the material gets damaged to create the interface.Basically no preknowledge is required other then how to safely use tools.
Iron cablesDepending on the thickness of the cable some tools might be required, but thin enough cables can be shaped by hand.Iron cable doesn't really have an obvious use other then to be deformed and reshaped, thus it is not seen as super precious.While the cables are very shapable in many forms, making things like joins or connections is difficult without also using other materials.Iron cable is highly interfacable with construction materials like carboard, wood and paper by "sewing" it though holes in the material.Experience helps a lot in making what you want fast with iron cables, but it is not a requirement.
BYORBYOR uses a very simpe input to output system that once discovered is rather intuative for people of all skill levels.BYOR contains a few prebuilt electronic parts which, while the 3D printed parts on them are rather low quality, would be somewhat more expensive to replace.The input to output system means that only one prefedined mapping is available for all partsThe parts had holes and split pins meant for interfacing with other materials. Given the strenth of the stepper and servo used it seems intended for cardboad.Some knowledge on what components can do might help a little, but in general no preknowledge is required.
Arduino grove sensor kitNot at all easy to use. It took the three of us almoat 10 minutes to figure out how to use it during the session.The manual states that the sensors should eventually be detached, but as they were electronics we were afraid it would damage the parts.Given the special connectors on the parts, this felt much more limiting then general arduino componets.The kit came with a shield that could be attached to an arduino, but our version did not include an arduino. Furthermore, this unnecesairily limits the specific microcontrollers that the parts can be connected to.Given that these are basically just arduino components with a weird extra component, quite a bit more knowledge is required if you don't want to be reading the manual a lot during tinkering.
MakeDo Cardboard screwsWhile the screws are a bit difficult to initially get into the cardboard, they are pretty straight forwards in use.The screws are of decent quality materials making them feel more precious then they actually are.The screws were very limited in their use, as they could only create non-rotating joints and needed a fair bit of support to do so. Split pins would be better in almost every way possible.The screws are very explicitly only made to work with cardboard and they are not likely to work with other materials.While no preknowledge is neccesairly required, it did take us a while to figure out that the spiky bits on the tools are for starting the holes through the cardboard.
General Arduino kitAs nothing of the connectors or code has been premade, using a more general arduino kit is not as easy.While the components on the breakout boards are "preassembeled" and thus shouldn't be damaged. Things like the cables and LEDs are easily replaceable and thus not very precious.The mouldability of a general arduino kit depends on what exact components you get in the kit. But as you get more components what is possible also expandsThe nice thing about more general kits is that they come with the much more universal jumper in/out pins. This means that you can attach almost any electrical component to it (be it through a MOSFET or relays)Of this whole list, this is probably the most preknowledge intensive material as you need to know how to code, how to design electical ciruts and sometimes even how certian communication protocols work to use it effectively.
