Session 3

The paper by Ju & Hoffmann (Design with motion in mind) is a very good source on using motion in your design(process). Which lessons would apply to your case?

The main lessons and challenges found by Ju & Hoffman can be summarized as follows:

lessons
- good movement means less focus is needed on antropomorphizing the robot
- starting from the movement instead of something to replicate allows for much more creative freedome
- simpler designs allow for faster and cheaper prototyping, especially oustide labs
- suspension of disbelief is a lot stronger when the character doesn't look like some natural thing we already know

Challenges
- designing the correct movement is hard and can require special experts or design methods to get right
- implementing the physical realisation of the movement might turn out to be too complex
- the apperance of the robot should also match its movements
- the designer needs to validate that the movements have their intended effect

The case we picked at the start of the course was to have Roes act as a fetching robot in an elderly care home. So either staff or one of the elders would ask Rose to get it, and the robot would go fetch it for them. Rose has some antropomorphisation, but its no where near the point where it can be described as humanoid. Thus, a good motion design is required to still replicate the trust and emotional connection that people would have with real humans in the scenario. The design of rose, with exception of its arm, is rather simple. But its arm is core to the scenario, so that lesson wasn't really applied to the robot. It does look like that during the design of Rose, or more specifically PAL Tiago, the places the arm could reach were their main priority, instead of making it look like something more familiar. However the addition of the head then slightly undoes this effect again, as in its current design it mostly resembles a coffee machine with a head, arm and screen. So it sits slightly between antropomorphised and non-natural. Which in turn would probably mean that it takes a bit longer for the residents of the carehome to come to accept the robot as a part of their life.

An often used tool for analysis of motion is the Laban Framework. can you give some good sources where it is applied to HRI, but (more importantly) can you also find one or two alternatives? (So, the question here is why 'Robot Motion' + 'Theatre' = Laban seems to be the main route)

From the summary by Konie [1], it seems that the Laban framework was designed to characterize human movements. There is a lot on how to classify the movements of humans. Not only in the way they position themselves and their limbs, but also how their posture and breathing should be classified. This is used by Sato et al. [2] to define their requirements for so called 'human symbiosis robotics'. Otero et al claim that the understanding of human body motions helps robots plan future goals [3]. The main alternative method that comes in the human motion recognition part of HMI are semantic labels such as in [4]. These are labels defined by the reseracher for motion recognition software to sort its detected motion patterns into. The main advantage of this method is that the reseracher/designer gets to define what motions to keep track of themselves and can optemize their system for a certain interaction. The downside however is that this also means that the designer will miss more subtle motions that might indicate important things for their system as it doesn't provide any real framework. These seem so common that I am unable to find any proper framework for analysis of human motion, only some that use motion to classify different things [5]

[1] R Konie, A BRIEF OVERVIEW OF LABAN MOVEMENT ANALYSIS, www.movementhasmeaning.com, 2011, Available: https://psychomotorischetherapie.info/website/wp-content/uploads/2015/10/LMA-Workshop-Sheet-Laban.pdf, last accessed 4-7-2025

[2] T. Sato, Y. Nishida, H. Mioguchi, Robotic room: Symbiosis with human through behavior media", Robotics and Autonomous Systems, Volume 18, Issues 1–2, July 1996, Pages 185-194. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/0921-8890(96)00004-8

[3] N. Otero, S. Knoop, C. L. Nehaniv, D. Syrdal, K. Dautenhahn, R. Dillmann, "Distribution and Recognition of Gestures in Human-Robot Interaction", ROMAN 2006 - The 15th IEEE International Symposium on Robot and Human Interactive Communication, Hatfield, UK, 2006, pp. 103-110, doi: 10.1109/ROMAN.2006.314402.

[4] E. S. Rahayu, E. M. Yuniarno, I. K. E. Purnama, M. H. Purnomo, "Human activity classification using deep learning based on 3D motion feature" Machine Learning with Applications, Volume 12, 15 June 2023, DOI: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.mlwa.2023.100461

[5] N. F. Troje, "Decomposing biological motion: A framework for analysis and synthesis of human gait patterns" in journal of vision, volume 2, issue 2 or 5, september 2002, DOI: https://doi.org/10.1167/2.5.2 (Note: the website states two different issues that the paper may be published in, I am unshure which it is)

Reflect on the following statement: To design a social robot and its communication we might need to take into account the way people parse behaviours and not necessarily replicate one-o-one human-like behaviour. Every robot can be interpreted as social if it displays animacy, agency (intentional behavior); which method would you use to design movement to achieve intentionality without relying on behaviours modelled on human behaviour?

I agree with the statement, as even within human communications there are a lot of differenecs from person to person, culture to cultre and neurotypical to neurodivergent. While the parsing of behaviours is also influenced by this, from my experiance it is done less so then the more subtle body language and speach connotations. As for how I would design the movement to acchieve intentionality I would mainly look at how animators deal with bringing non-living objects to life. They tend to focus a lot more on the "impact" or "energy" with which a movement is performed then exactly how its done. Emotions like happiness could for example be expressed through more bouncy movement while anger is more sharp and fast movements and sadness are slow languishing movements. If the robot has something that looks like eyes, having them constantly look at the object it is manipulating can indicate a focus on the task. And through gestures like pointing, nodding or shaking communication can be established even without language processing. While these take inspiration from human/animal like expressiveness, they don't 1:1 coppy them. Rather they try to get to the core of why the expressions work and then see if they can apply that on whatever object they are animating.

So far we have focussed mainly on motion as a means for expression. Can you find fundamental insights (such as the Laban framework for motion) that gives you a starting point for sound design? or haptics? or morphology?

The paper by [6] proposes a sound design framework that combines Ethnomethodology and Conversation Analysis (EMCA) and interaction design into one. By utilizing video data, they claim EMCA can inform the interaction design through things likve wizzard-of-ozing and voice overs. At the same time the video recorded prototyping sessions feed back into the video data for EMCA. Transcripts of these recordings can supposedly also help the team reflect on their work and understand which parts are most crucial to their design. For haptics design the most important thing to keep in mind will most likely be the two point discrimination [7] threshold of the area you want to apply the haptics in. Furthermore, there are also different kinds of touch sensing that can happen [8]. It can be beneficial to know what kind of sensation you are aiming for and optimize the placement for that.

[6] H. R. M. Pelikan, "Robot Sound in Interaction : Analyzing and Designing Sound for Human-Robot Coordination", 2023, DOI: 10.3384/9789180751179.

[7] P. Rea, "Two-Point Discrimination Test", Essential clinical anatomy of the nervous system, chapter 8.2.3.2.1, 2015.

[8] K Sönderlund Leifler, "Human sense of touch consists of 16 unique types of nerve cells" https://liu.se/en/news-item/human-sense-of-touch-consists-of-16-unique-types-of-nerve-cells

If a large part of the design work seems to evolve around making robots sociable, what would the design space of a (deliberately) anti-social robot look like?

Depends on if with anti-social you mean not following social norms or non-sociable. The not following social rules still requires basically the exact same design work, only now instead of going for all the best options, you go for all the worst. If a non-sociable robot is meant, the design simply becomes an engineering question of oplimalisation of the task.
